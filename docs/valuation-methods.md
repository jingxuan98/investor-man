# Valuation methods, in plain English

This is a reference for the 10 valuation models on the Value tab, plus the
Composite. It's written for a growth investor, not a finance professional —
every formula below is described in words first, then given exactly as coded,
with a file citation so you can check it yourself. Nothing here is invented:
if the code changes, this doc is out of date and should be fixed to match it,
not the other way around.

All 10 methods and the composite live in `lib/finance/valuation.ts`
(`computeValuation`). The growth-fit chips referenced throughout are defined
in `components/ValueTable.tsx` (`GROWTH_FIT`).

---

## The shared building block: the three-stage 20-year path

Every "20Y" method (DCF-20, DFCF-20, DNI-20, Revenue DCF) projects its base
number forward for exactly 20 years in three stages, then discounts each
year's cash flow back to today at the discount rate (WACC). There is **no
terminal value tacked on after year 20** — the 20-year window is the entire
valuation.

- **Years 1–5:** grow at the full growth seed `g`.
- **Years 6–10:** growth fades to `0.7 × g`.
- **Years 11–20:** growth settles at the terminal rate (4% by default).

```
threeStagePath(g, gT) = [g, g, g, g, g, 0.7g, 0.7g, 0.7g, 0.7g, 0.7g,
                         gT, gT, gT, gT, gT, gT, gT, gT, gT, gT]

PV = Σ_{t=1..20}  cf_t / (1 + WACC)^t     where cf_t compounds forward
                                           by the stage growth rate each year
```
(`threeStagePath` / `threeStagePv`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:65-88`)

A faster grower (higher `g`) gets a mechanically bigger year-1–5 boost, which
is why methods built on this path are tagged **GROWTH** below.

## 1. DCF-20 (Discounted Operating Cash Flow) — GROWTH

**What it measures:** the present value of 20 years of the company's
operating cash flow (cash actually generated by the business, before
investing/financing), adjusted for net cash or debt.

**Formula (as implemented):**
```
base   = TTM operating cash flow (or latest fiscal year if TTM missing)
PV     = threeStagePv(base, growth seed, WACC, terminal growth)
equity = PV + (cash − total debt)
price  = equity / shares outstanding
```
Null if the base is missing or ≤ 0, or if the net-debt adjustment pushes
equity ≤ 0 (never shows a negative implied price).
(`dcfModel`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:108-126`, called at `:229`)

**Inputs and source:**
- Base: `financialData.operatingCashflow` (TTM) from Yahoo, falling back to
  the latest fiscal-year `operatingCashFlow` derived from Yahoo's
  `fundamentalsTimeSeries` statements (`lib/data/yahoo.ts`).
- Net debt: latest-year `totalDebt`/`cash`, preferring `financialData`'s
  current balance-sheet snapshot over the last annual filing.
- Growth seed and WACC: see "Auto growth seed & WACC" below.

**Why GROWTH:** the full growth seed compounds directly onto the cash-flow
base for 5 years before fading — a high-growth company's higher seed flows
straight into a bigger present value.

**Blind spot:** the growth-decay schedule (100% → 70% → terminal) is rigid
and assumed, not derived from the company's actual trajectory; it also
trusts the 5-year historical growth seed to be a reasonable proxy for the
next 5–10 years, which can overstate durability for a company riding a
temporary tailwind — or understate one still early in an S-curve.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Every knob in this model leans somewhere.
The growth seed is floored at 2% and capped at 30%: the cap is
**CONSERVATIVE** for a hypergrowth name — NVDA's real ~67% 5-year revenue
CAGR gets clipped all the way down to 30%, mechanically understating its
20-year cash-flow path — while the 2% floor is mildly **AGGRESSIVE** in the
other direction, crediting a genuinely shrinking business with 2%/year
growth it isn't actually delivering. The stage-2/stage-3 schedule (70% of
the seed for years 6–10, a flat 4% for years 11–20) is a mildly
**AGGRESSIVE** tail, since 4% sits at or above long-run GDP growth — every
company, however mediocre, is credited with keeping pace with the whole
economy for a full decade. Crediting **no value beyond year 20** is
**strongly CONSERVATIVE**: a durable business worth owning past year 20 is
worth strictly more than this model shows, and that omission penalizes the
most durable moats the hardest. The discount rate — CAPM-based, capped at
12%, floored at 4% — is **AGGRESSIVE** at the 12% cap for high-beta names,
treating a genuinely volatile stock as no riskier than a moderately volatile
one and so discounting its cash flows less than textbook CAPM would. The
base (TTM operating cash flow, or latest FY if TTM is missing) is neutral —
always the most current figure available, never a stale snapshot.

**vs the reference product:** all four of the knobs above — the 2%/30% growth band,
the 0.7×/4% stage schedule, the no-terminal-value rule, and the CAPM
5.5%-ERP/12%-cap/4%-floor discount rate — are identical to the reference product's own
calculator inputs, verified directly from their calculator (their TSLA,
NVDA, and DDOG entries all show the same 12.00% discount-rate ceiling).
Combined with an always-current base rather than a stale multi-year-old
snapshot, running our engine on the same ticker reproduces their live
calculator's output closely: a 0.93–1.08 ratio verified across 16 of 20
spot-checked tickers. The 4 exceptions are structural, not tuning drift —
banks, where this style of model isn't designed to apply and both sites
print nonsense by design, and foreign ADRs, where the two sites' underlying
data vendors simply disagree on the raw financials. For DCF-20, in short: our
numbers are effectively the reference product's engine run fresh on today's data.

## 2. DFCF-20 (Discounted Free Cash Flow) — ALL-ROUNDER

**What it measures:** the same 20-year present-value math as DCF-20, but on
free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capital expenditure) — the cash
left over after the company pays for its own equipment/investment.

**Formula:** identical to DCF-20 with `base = TTM free cash flow` (Yahoo
`financialData.freeCashflow`, or `operatingCashFlow − capex` from the latest
fiscal year), net-debt adjustment included.
(`dcfModel`, called at `lib/finance/valuation.ts:230`)

**Why ALL-ROUNDER (not GROWTH):** it uses the same growth-sensitive 20-year
path as DCF-20, so it does reward growth — but free cash flow is what's left
*after* heavy reinvestment, and a fast grower plowing cash into capacity can
show thin or even negative FCF in the base year, muting or nulling the whole
model. It rewards growth when the growth is capital-light, and undersells it
when the growth is capital-intensive — hence a middle-of-the-road tag rather
than a clean GROWTH label.

**Blind spot:** a single year's free cash flow is a noisy foundation
(one large capex project can swing it), and the same rigid growth-decay
schedule as DCF-20 applies.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Identical framework to DCF-20 — same 2%
floor / 30% cap on the growth seed (**CONSERVATIVE** for hypergrowth,
mildly **AGGRESSIVE** for shrinkers), same 0.7×-then-4% stage schedule
(mildly **AGGRESSIVE** tail), same **strongly CONSERVATIVE** no-value-beyond-
year-20 rule, and the same CAPM discount rate capped at 12% / floored at 4%
(**AGGRESSIVE** for high-beta names). The one difference from DCF-20 is the
base itself: TTM free cash flow (operating cash flow minus capex) rather
than raw operating cash flow — still a neutral, always-current choice, but
a noisier one, since a single heavy capex year can swing it.

**vs the reference product:** same identical growth-seed and discount-rate tuning as
DCF-20, and the same always-current basis, so it inherits the same
verified match — a 0.93–1.08 ratio across 16 of 20 tickers, with the same
bank/ADR exceptions. We are effectively the reference product's engine, run on fresh
free-cash-flow figures.

## 3. DNI-20 (Discounted Net Income) — GROWTH

**What it measures:** the present value of 20 years of the company's
accounting profit (net income), with **no net-debt adjustment** — this is a
pure earnings-power valuation.

**Formula:**
```
base  = TTM net income (trailing EPS × shares), or latest FY net income
PV    = threeStagePv(base, growth seed, WACC, terminal growth)
price = PV / shares outstanding          (no cash/debt adjustment)
```
(`dcfModel` with `adjustNetDebt = false`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:231`)

**Inputs:** TTM net income is derived as `trailingEps × shares` (Yahoo
`defaultKeyStatistics.trailingEps`); falls back to the latest annual
`netIncome` statement.

**Why GROWTH:** same three-stage growth path as DCF-20, applied to earnings
instead of cash flow — a higher growth seed produces a proportionally larger
20-year earnings stream.

**Blind spot:** net income can be swung by one-off items, tax rate changes,
or impairments in the base year; it also ignores how much capital the
company must reinvest to keep growing (unlike a cash-flow-based method).

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Same growth-seed cap/floor, stage-2/3
fade schedule, and no-terminal-value rule as DCF-20 — **CONSERVATIVE** cap
for hypergrowth, mildly **AGGRESSIVE** floor and 4% tail, **strongly
CONSERVATIVE** 20-year cutoff, and the same **AGGRESSIVE** 12%-cap /
4%-floor CAPM discount rate. Base is TTM net income (or latest FY), also
neutral and always-current.

**vs the reference product:** identical growth-seed and discount-rate inputs to
DCF-20 and DFCF-20, matching the reference product's calculator formula and inheriting
the same 0.93–1.08 verified-match band (16 of 20 tickers).

## 4. H-Model DCF — MATURE/VALUE

**What it measures:** a one-formula shortcut version of a DCF that assumes
growth doesn't hold at its current rate for years and then suddenly drop —
instead it fades in a straight line from today's growth rate down to a
long-run terminal rate over a fixed "half-life" window.

**Formula (as implemented):**
```
V = FCF₀ × [(1 + gT) + H × (g₀ − gT)] / (WACC − gT)
price = V / shares outstanding     (NO cash/debt adjustment)
```
where `FCF₀` = TTM free cash flow, `g₀` = growth seed, `gT` = terminal
growth, `H` = the H half-life knob (default 4 years), `WACC` = discount
rate. Null if FCF₀ ≤ 0, if `WACC ≤ terminal growth`, or if a steep assumed
decline (`g₀ ≪ gT`) makes the numerator ≤ 0.
(`lib/finance/valuation.ts:233-254`)

**Why MATURE/VALUE:** the model is built around the premise that growth is
*already fading* toward a steady terminal rate within a handful of years —
exactly the profile of a mature compounder, not a company early in a growth
runway. A fast grower's real high-growth window is typically much longer
than the fade the H-model assumes, so it tends to understate one.

**Blind spot:** the linear fade-in-time assumption rarely matches how real
growth curves actually decay; and uniquely among the DCF family here, it
applies **no net cash/debt adjustment at all** — two companies with
identical FCF but very different balance sheets get the same H-model value.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** The growth seed feeds this model's
starting rate `g₀`, so the same cap/floor logic applies: **CONSERVATIVE**
for a hypergrower (clipped to 30%), mildly **AGGRESSIVE** for a shrinker
(floored at 2%). The fade target `gT` is the same fixed 4% terminal rate —
still a mildly **AGGRESSIVE** assumption, since it credits every company
with eventually settling at a GDP-level growth rate rather than fading
toward something lower. Unlike the 20-year methods, this model has no
explicit "nothing beyond year 20" cutoff — its closed form already bakes in
an implicit perpetual tail through the `(WACC − gT)` denominator — so the
**strongly CONSERVATIVE** no-terminal-value point that applies to DCF-20,
DFCF-20, DNI-20, and Revenue DCF does not apply here in the same way; if
anything, the built-in infinite horizon pulls this model in the opposite
direction. The discount rate uses the same CAPM 12%-cap / 4%-floor, still
**AGGRESSIVE** for high-beta names.

**vs the reference product:** the growth-seed and discount-rate inputs feeding this
model are identical to the reference product's — the same 2%/30% growth band and the
same 12%-cap/4%-floor CAPM discount rate verified across their calculator
inputs. This method isn't part of the specific 0.93–1.08 ratio check (that
check covers the always-current, TTM-basis 20-year methods), but the
growth/discount assumptions feeding it are the same ones verified there.

## 5. EV/EBITDA (Multiples) — ALL-ROUNDER

**What it measures:** what the business would be worth if the market priced
it at its own typical historical Enterprise-Value-to-EBITDA multiple.

**Formula:**
```
EV_t (per historical year) = yearEndPrice_t × shares_t + debt_t − cash_t
ratio_t = EV_t / EBITDA_t
own multiple = median(ratio_t across all available years)
fair EV = own multiple × latest EBITDA − latest debt + latest cash
price   = fair EV / shares outstanding
```
This is the one multiple method that **always uses the company's own
history**, never an industry/sector override — the reference site's live
calculator does the same (own-history reproduced its behavior on ≈9/12
calibration tickers within 5%; a sector figure did not improve on that).
(`medianMultiple`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:132-147`; the "always own-history"
choice is applied at `:261` — no override argument is passed to
`multipleModel` for this key, unlike EV/Revenue and P/FCF below)

**Inputs:** EBITDA = `operatingIncome + depreciation` (approximation, from
Yahoo's annual statements); year-end price from the 5-year monthly close
history; debt/cash from each year's balance sheet.

**Why ALL-ROUNDER:** it's anchored to the company's own historical multiple
rather than to a forward growth assumption, so it doesn't project a growth
story at all — it just asks "what if you re-rated to your own past
average?" That works reasonably whether the company is a grower or a
compounder, but doesn't specifically reward or punish either.

**Blind spot:** it's purely backward-looking — if the market has
consistently over- or under-priced the stock, this method just re-applies
that same mispricing rather than correcting for it.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** This is the one multiple method with no
industry/sector override at all — it always re-rates the company to its
own trailing 5-year EV/EBITDA history. That's **AGGRESSIVE** specifically
for ex-bubble names: a stock whose own past multiple was inflated by a
since-popped bubble, or a since-faded growth narrative, carries that
inflated multiple forward as its "fair" value instead of being pulled back
toward a sober sector average.

**vs the reference product:** the industry/sector table used by the other multiple
methods is calibrated to the reference product's values, and always-own-history is the
choice the reference product's own live calculator also makes for this specific
multiple — reproducing their behavior on about 9 of 12 calibration tickers
within 5%, with a sector-based alternative tested and rejected because it
didn't improve on that fit.

## 6. EV/Revenue (Multiples) — GROWTH

**What it measures:** what the business's sales would be worth priced at the
typical multiple for its industry or sector.

**Formula:**
```
multiple = industry override, else sector-table value, else own-history median
fair EV  = multiple × latest revenue − latest debt + latest cash
price    = fair EV / shares outstanding
```
(`multipleModel`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:149-173`, called at `:265`)

**Multiple resolution order** (`resolveMultiples`, `lib/finance/valuation.ts:50-57`):
1. **Industry** — `INDUSTRY_MULTIPLES[s.industry]` (Yahoo
   `assetProfile.industry`), e.g. `"Internet Content & Information": { evRev: 5.3 }`.
2. **Sector** — `SECTOR_MULTIPLES[s.sector]` (Yahoo `assetProfile.sector`),
   e.g. `"Communication Services": { evRev: 3.0 }`, used for any field the
   industry entry doesn't define.
3. **Own-history** — the same per-year EV/metric median used by EV/EBITDA,
   if neither industry nor sector is known.

This two-level lookup exists because the reference site's real multiple
table is industry-level, not sector-level: Meta ("Internet Content &
Information") and Verizon ("Telecom Services") are both "Communication
Services" companies, but the reference site implies ~5.3× EV/Revenue for one
and ~3.0× for the other — a single sector number can't reproduce both.

**Why GROWTH:** revenue-based multiples work even for companies that aren't
profitable yet (or barely so) — which describes a lot of high-growth
companies — so this is often the *only* multiple method that produces a
number at all for a young grower, and industry-level revenue multiples for
growth-heavy industries (software, internet, semis) run materially higher
than the broader sector average.

**Blind spot:** it says nothing about whether that revenue ever converts to
profit — a company with a rich revenue multiple and deeply negative margins
can look "cheap" by this method while burning cash indefinitely.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Because industry-level revenue multiples
for growth-heavy industries (software, internet, semis) run well above the
broader sector average, using the industry table first is **CONSERVATIVE**
for premium franchises — a company like NVDA gets priced at "ordinary
semis" multiples rather than the richer, growth-justified multiple its own
fundamentals might deserve. The same fixed-table approach is
**AGGRESSIVE** for low-quality names in the same industry bucket, which get
credited with the same multiple as their stronger industry peers
regardless of their own weaker fundamentals.

**vs the reference product:** the industry/sector table itself is calibrated directly
to the reference product's implied multiples — the Meta-vs-Verizon split (both
"Communication Services" by sector, but Meta's "Internet Content &
Information" industry and Verizon's "Telecom Services" industry imply very
different EV/Revenue multiples on the reference product) is exactly why this table is
built at the industry level rather than the sector level.

## 7. P/FCF (Multiples) — MATURE/VALUE

**What it measures:** what the business's free cash flow would be worth
priced like the average company in its industry/sector (a straight price
multiple, not an enterprise-value one — no debt/cash adjustment).

**Formula:**
```
multiple = industry override, else sector-table value, else own-history median
fair value (equity) = multiple × latest free cash flow
price = fair value / shares outstanding
```
Same industry → sector → own-history resolution as EV/Revenue
(`resolveMultiples`), but `useEV = false` — no net-debt adjustment.
(`multipleModel`, called at `lib/finance/valuation.ts:269`)

**Why MATURE/VALUE:** it prices the company off a *fixed* multiple applied
to today's free cash flow, with no explicit growth path — the multiple
itself is the only place growth expectations can show up, and the
calibrated industry/sector P/FCF figures (20–31×) are pitched at a broad
peer average, not tailored to an individual company's own growth rate. For
a company still burning or barely generating FCF, this method also simply
has nothing to work with (null).

**Blind spot:** it's a straight equity multiple (no EV adjustment), so it
ignores leverage entirely — two companies with identical FCF but very
different debt loads get valued the same.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Same industry → sector → own-history
resolution as EV/Revenue, applied to free cash flow instead of revenue:
**CONSERVATIVE** for premium franchises, whose superior FCF conversion or
growth story gets compressed to a peer-average multiple, and
**AGGRESSIVE** for low-quality names in the same bucket, who get the same
multiple as their stronger peers.

**vs the reference product:** same relationship as EV/Revenue — the industry/sector
P/FCF table (20–31× depending on industry) is calibrated to the reference product's
values.

## 8. Revenue DCF — GROWTH

**What it measures:** projects the company's sales 20 years out on the same
three-stage growth path, applies a gradually expanding profit margin to each
year's projected revenue, and discounts those implied profits back to today.

**Formula (as implemented):**
```
m0 = latest net income / latest revenue          (starting margin)
for year i = 1..20:
    revenue_i = revenue_{i-1} × (1 + stage growth rate)
    margin_i  = min(m0 + marginExpansion × i, m0 + 0.10)   (10pp expansion cap)
    pv       += (revenue_i × margin_i) / (1 + WACC)^i
price = pv / shares outstanding
```
Uses the latest fiscal-year revenue (not TTM) as the starting point; net
income base follows the same TTM-or-FY preference as DNI-20.
(`lib/finance/valuation.ts:273-295`)

**Inputs:** latest fiscal-year revenue and net income (for the starting
margin), the growth seed and terminal growth (three-stage path), and the
Margin Expansion knob (default 0, user-adjustable in %/year).

**Why GROWTH:** it projects the top line directly — which is the metric
that best captures a young company's actual growth story — and lets margin
expand as the business scales, rather than assuming today's (possibly
thin or negative) profit margin holds forever.

**Blind spot:** margin expansion is capped at a fixed +10 percentage points
over the whole 20-year window regardless of the company's real margin
ceiling, and there's still no terminal value beyond year 20 — a company that
would only become truly profitable in year 25 gets nothing credited for
that.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Shares the same growth-seed cap/floor,
stage-2/3 fade schedule, and no-terminal-value rule as the other 20-year
methods — **CONSERVATIVE** cap for hypergrowth, mildly **AGGRESSIVE** floor
and 4% tail, **strongly CONSERVATIVE** 20-year cutoff. Discount rate uses
the same CAPM 12%-cap / 4%-floor, **AGGRESSIVE** for high-beta names. (The
margin-expansion knob and its fixed +10pp cap are a separate assumption not
covered by the growth/discount tuning notes above.)

**vs the reference product:** same growth-seed and discount-rate inputs as every
other 20-year method in this doc, so it inherits the same identical-tuning
relationship to the reference product's calculator and the same always-current basis
as the family it belongs to.

## 9. PEG-implied — GROWTH

**What it measures:** the classic "fair P/E equals the growth rate" rule
(a PEG ratio of exactly 1.0) — a company growing profits 20%/year
"deserves" roughly a 20× earnings multiple.

**Formula (as implemented):**
```
gRaw   = manual growth override, else 5Y net-income CAGR, else growth seed
g100   = clamp(gRaw × 100, 0, 100)     (growth expressed as a P/E number, capped at 100)
price  = trailing EPS × g100
```
Null if trailing EPS ≤ 0 or `gRaw ≤ 0`.
(`lib/finance/valuation.ts:297-312`)

**Inputs:** trailing EPS (Yahoo `defaultKeyStatistics.trailingEps`); growth
input is the **5-year net-income CAGR** (`pegGrowth`, see below) — different
from every other method's growth seed, which uses revenue CAGR — unless the
user has manually set the Growth knob, in which case that wins.

**Why GROWTH:** it's *defined* by the growth rate — there's no method here
more directly a bet on growth than "your fair multiple literally equals your
growth number."

**Blind spot:** the sanity cap of 100 is generous — an 80%-grower
mechanically implies an 80× P/E, which may not be a realistic multiple even
if the company really is growing that fast; also entirely ignores the
balance sheet (no cash/debt adjustment) and assumes the trailing 5-year
growth rate will persist.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** The growth input itself — the 5-year
net-income CAGR — is uncapped; only the final derived P/E number (`g100`)
carries a sanity cap, at 100. That's **AGGRESSIVE** for early-cycle
earnings explosions: a company coming off a depressed or near-zero earnings
base (NVDA is the reference case) can post a triple-digit net-income CAGR,
and PEG's "fair P/E = growth rate" logic takes that number at face value —
up to the cap — rather than discounting it as a temporary base-effect
artifact.

**vs the reference product:** the formula matches the reference product's own — uncapped
net-income CAGR feeding directly into a 1:1 fair-P/E. This is the same
formula fidelity already noted above: it back-solves to the reference product's growth
input closely on real tickers (GOOGL 24.8 vs. an actual ~25.2, NVDA ≈99,
KO 9.7 vs. an actual ~10.0).

*(Note: an earlier note about this method described the sanity cap as 60;
the code and the formula section above both confirm the cap is 100 — the
tuning assessment here uses the verified figure.)*

## 10. Graham Revised — MATURE/VALUE

**What it measures:** Benjamin Graham's classic revised formula for
intrinsic value: earnings times a factor built from a fixed base plus twice
the growth rate, scaled against a fixed "AAA bond yield" proxy.

**Formula (as implemented):**
```
g100  = clamp(growth seed × 100, 0, 30)     (growth capped at 30, not 100)
Y     = 5.0                                  (fixed AAA-proxy yield, not live)
price = EPS × (8.5 + 2 × g100) × 4.4 / Y
```
(`lib/finance/valuation.ts:314-328`)

**Why MATURE/VALUE:** the growth term is capped at 30% (far tighter than
PEG's 100 cap), and the formula's whole shape — `8.5 + 2g` — comes from a
mid-20th-century valuation era calibrated to steady industrial-era
earnings growth. It mechanically compresses the value of anything growing
faster than the cap allows.

**Blind spot:** the fixed `Y = 5.0` doesn't move with actual interest
rates, and the 30%-cap growth term means two companies growing 35% and 90%
get treated identically — the formula simply cannot distinguish exceptional
growth from merely strong growth.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Inherently **CONSERVATIVE** — the
formula's whole shape (`8.5 + 2g`) and its fixed AAA-bond-yield proxy are a
mid-20th-century value-investing construct, calibrated to steady
industrial-era earnings growth. It will structurally undervalue — and
often outright dislike — every fast-growing SaaS or software name pointed
at it, growth cap or not.

**vs the reference product:** the formula matches exactly — it's reference-verified
on all 12 calibration tickers, back-solving to a fixed `Y = 5.0` on every
one of them. Unlike the DCF family's 0.93–1.08 *ratio* match, this is an
exact formula match: same shape, same fixed yield proxy, same growth cap.

## Composite

**What it measures:** a single blended fair-value estimate that discounts
the two most extreme model outputs, so one broken or unusually
optimistic/pessimistic method doesn't dominate the number.

**How it trims (as implemented):**
```
valid = every model's value that isn't null
if count(valid) >= 5:
    sorted  = valid, ascending
    trimmed = sorted with the single lowest and single highest dropped
    composite = mean(trimmed)
    range = { min: sorted[0], max: sorted[last] }   (the FULL range, untrimmed)
else:
    composite = null   ("n/a — fewer than 5 valid methods")
```
(`lib/finance/valuation.ts:330-339`)

Note the displayed **range** is the full min/max across all valid models —
only the composite average itself drops the extremes.

**How aggressive is our tuning?** Of every choice on this page, this is the
one that isn't aggressive or conservative in either direction — it's the
sanest default available. By dropping the single wildest optimist *and* the
single wildest pessimist before averaging, the Composite makes sure that
neither an over-aggressive method (PEG on an earnings explosion, EV/Revenue
on a premium-industry name) nor an over-conservative one (Graham, H-Model)
can single-handedly drag the blended number away from the other eight
methods' consensus.

## How the auto growth seed and WACC are derived

Both are computed in `lib/finance/assumptions.ts` and used unless the user
overrides them with the Growth/WACC knobs on the Value tab.

**Growth seed (`autoNormalGrowth`):**
```
g = revenueCagr5y(s), clamped to [2%, 30%]; defaults to 5% if no history at all
```
`revenueCagr5y` prefers a longer SEC EDGAR window (up to 6 fiscal-year
endpoints / 5 year-over-year transitions of revenue) over Yahoo's own
statements, which cap out around 4 years. The EDGAR window is only trusted
if it has at least 4 non-null revenue points in it (the "min-span guard") —
otherwise it falls back to whatever Yahoo years are available.
(`revenueCagr5y`, `edgarWindow`, `lib/finance/assumptions.ts:23-48`)

**PEG's growth input is different** (`pegGrowth`): it uses the same
EDGAR-preferred, min-span-guarded window logic, but on **net income**
instead of revenue, falling back to the revenue CAGR if the net-income CAGR
can't be computed (e.g. a loss year in the window).
(`lib/finance/assumptions.ts:53-57`)

**WACC (`autoWacc`):**
```
WACC = 10-yr Treasury yield + beta × 5.5%,  clamped to [4%, 12%]
```
Beta defaults to 1.0 if Yahoo doesn't report one; the risk-free rate comes
from a live `^TNX` quote (default 4.2% if that fetch fails).
(`lib/finance/assumptions.ts:75-78`)

**Terminal growth** is *not* auto-derived from company data — it's a fixed
4% default (a "steady-state" assumption), overridable by the user, that
drives both stage 3 (years 11–20) of every three-stage projection and the
H-model's fade target.

## Which methods to trust for growth stocks

| Method | Growth-fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| DCF-20 | GROWTH | Full growth seed compounds directly onto operating cash flow for 5 years |
| DNI-20 | GROWTH | Same growth path applied to earnings; no capital-intensity check |
| Revenue DCF | GROWTH | Projects the top line directly and lets margins expand as the business scales |
| PEG-implied | GROWTH | Fair multiple *is* the growth rate — the most direct growth bet here |
| EV/Revenue | GROWTH | Works even pre-profitability; industry multiples for growth sectors already run high |
| DFCF-20 | ALL-ROUNDER | Growth-sensitive, but heavy reinvestment can starve the FCF base it runs on |
| EV/EBITDA | ALL-ROUNDER | No growth projection at all — just re-rates to the company's own historical multiple |
| H-Model DCF | MATURE/VALUE | Assumes growth is already fading fast toward a steady terminal rate |
| P/FCF | MATURE/VALUE | Fixed peer multiple with no explicit growth path; ignores leverage |
| Graham Revised | MATURE/VALUE | Growth capped at 30%; formula era-calibrated to steady industrial earners |

As a rule of thumb: for a fast-growing, possibly-not-yet-profitable
company, lean on **DCF-20, Revenue DCF, PEG, and EV/Revenue** — they either
project growth directly or don't require current profitability. Treat
**H-Model, P/FCF, and Graham** as a sanity floor rather than a fair-value
target for such a company — they're built around assumptions (fast-fading
growth, peer-average multiples, capped growth terms) that structurally
undervalue anything growing quickly. The Composite already down-weights
whichever single method is most out of line, but with only 10 inputs, a
cluster of 3 MATURE/VALUE methods can still pull it below what a
growth-focused view would consider fair.

## Tuning summary & reference-product comparison

Every knob in this engine leans conservative, aggressive, or neutral
somewhere — none of them are "wrong," but none of them are free of a point
of view either. Here's every method's lean in one place, plus how each one
sits against the reference product — the reference site whose live calculator this
engine was built to reproduce.

| Method | Tuning lean | vs reference product |
|---|---|---|
| DCF-20 | Conservative growth cap (30%) for hypergrowth; mildly aggressive growth floor (2%), 4% terminal tail, and 12% WACC cap | Identical inputs — effectively their engine run fresh (0.93–1.08 on 16/20 tickers) |
| DFCF-20 | Same as DCF-20 | Same — identical inputs, same verified band |
| DNI-20 | Same as DCF-20 | Same — identical inputs, same verified band |
| H-Model DCF | Same growth/WACC leans as DCF-20; no explicit 20-year cutoff (implicit perpetual tail in the formula itself) | Same growth-seed/WACC inputs; no separate ratio check performed |
| EV/EBITDA | Aggressive for ex-bubble names — always re-rates to the company's own (possibly inflated) history | Own-history choice matches the reference product's live calculator (~9/12 within 5%) |
| EV/Revenue | Conservative for premium franchises, aggressive for low-quality industry peers | Industry/sector table calibrated directly to the reference product's values |
| P/FCF | Same lean as EV/Revenue | Same — table calibrated to the reference product |
| Revenue DCF | Same as DCF-20 family, plus a capped +10pp margin-expansion assumption | Same — identical growth/WACC inputs |
| PEG-implied | Aggressive for early-cycle earnings explosions — growth input uncapped, only the derived P/E is capped (at 100) | Formula matches the reference product's (NVDA ≈99, GOOGL 24.8 vs. ~25.2, KO 9.7 vs. ~10.0) |
| Graham Revised | Inherently conservative — 1970s-era formula, will undervalue every SaaS name | Exact formula match — back-solves to Y=5.0 on all 12 calibration tickers |
| Composite | Neutral by design — drops the single wildest optimist and pessimist before averaging | n/a — an internal aggregation, not a figure the reference product itself publishes |

**Net effect: slightly conservative overall for high-quality growth names,
slightly generous for volatile ones (WACC cap).** The 30% growth-seed cap,
the flat no-terminal-value-beyond-year-20 rule, and the industry-multiple
tables for EV/Revenue and P/FCF all systematically understate the very
best, highest-growth compounders — a real NVDA or a real hypergrowth SaaS
name is worth more than these methods will show, because the model simply
refuses to extrapolate their real growth rate or credit them for surviving
past year 20. At the same time, the 12% cap on the CAPM-derived discount
rate is generous to genuinely volatile, high-beta stocks — it discounts
their cash flows less than an uncapped CAPM calculation would, quietly
inflating the fair value of exactly the names that are riskiest to hold.
Both leans are deliberate design choices matched to the reference product's own
calculator, not bugs — but they mean the Composite number is best read as
"conservative-to-fair for quality growth, a bit generous for high-beta
speculation" rather than as a perfectly unbiased midpoint.
